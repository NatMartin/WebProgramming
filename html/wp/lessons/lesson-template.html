Programming requires two very different kinds of knowledge: concrete practical knowledge about how to accomplish specific tasks and theoretical knowledge about what the tasks accomplish. It is like learning to ride a bicycle: you need to know how to balance and pedal, but you also need to know to ride on the side of the road. Practical knowledge you can only learn by doing; it gives you the meanings of the words. Theoretical knowledge you can only learn by reading and talking; it gives you the ways the words fit together.

This section of the web site gives you the theoretical knowledge. It is broken into four sections. The first section covers the development of computing and the World Wide Web. This will describe how digital computers have developed since their first appearance. This part of the class will show you how user interfaces have changes under the influence of the hardware available and the demands placed on that hardware.

The second section covers the programming of the World Wide Web. This is the theoretical aspects of the practical work you will have been doing in the labs. It will cover the particular choices made by the labs and the alternatives to these choices.

The third section covers techniques for programming. It teaches how to develop programs that can survive in the real world, that is, programs that the programming can be confident that they do what is intended and can change when they need to.

The fourth section covers techniques for making sure that when they program do what is intended, they are also doing things that their users want them to. That is, the third section teaches how to write programs that do what they are supposed to do; the fours section teaches how to write programs that do what people want them to do. These are very different things: A program that only adds 2+2 works correctly when it produces the answer 4, but it is unlikely to be used by anyone. This section will show how to find out what people want to do.
